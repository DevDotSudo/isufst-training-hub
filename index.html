Group 1

Title : Fish Decline

What happens if fishing overexploitation continues to occur? If fishing overexploitation continues, fish populations will decline at an unsustainable rate, potentially leading to the collapse of certain species. This disruption in marine ecosystems can have cascading effects, as the loss of key species affects food chains and biodiversity. Overexploitation can also harm the livelihoods of communities dependent on fishing, as resources become scarcer and harder to access. Additionally, it may result in long-term environmental damage, making it more difficult for fish populations to recover, even if fishing practices are eventually regulated.

Group 2

Title : Impact of Mangrove Planting on Seagrass Ecosystem

Mangrove planting can have a positive impact on seagrass ecosystems by improving water quality through sediment stabilization and reducing nutrient runoff. The roots of mangrove trees provide shelter and enhance biodiversity, creating a favorable environment for juvenile marine species that also rely on seagrass meadows. However, excessive mangrove planting in areas where they are not naturally suited could lead to competition for space and light, potentially harming the seagrass. When appropriately integrated, mangrove forests and seagrass meadows can work together to support a diverse and healthy coastal ecosystem.

Group 3

Title : Behavioural Pattern of Marine Fishes under the relation of
Natural Calamity Disturbances before and after.

This project seeks to simulate the behavior of a school of marine fish in response to various disturbances, such as predator presence or environmental changes. It will model the fish's movement patterns, social interactions, and decision-making before, during, and after the disturbance. By analyzing these behaviors, the project aims to better understand how fish schools adapt to disruptions and maintain their cohesion.

Group 4

Title : THE EFFECTS OF NET SIZES TO NON-TARGET
AND BYCATCH SPECIES

The size of fishing nets can significantly impact non-target and bycatch species, as smaller mesh sizes often capture a wider range of organisms, including juveniles and non-target species. This results in higher rates of bycatch, which can harm populations of vulnerable species and disrupt marine ecosystems. Larger mesh sizes may help reduce bycatch by allowing smaller organisms to escape, but they may also reduce the overall catch of target species, potentially impacting fisheries' economic viability. The use of selective fishing gear and modifications to net sizes can mitigate bycatch and support more sustainable fishing practices.
